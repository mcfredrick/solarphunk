title: AI, Proforestation, and the Economics of Regeneration
---

In a time of climate emergency, the concept of proforestation invites us to radically rethink our relationship to forests. Rather than managing woodlands solely for human use—whether for timber, carbon offsets, or recreation—proforestation calls for letting forests grow old, complex, and self-willed. By relinquishing some control, we allow for a deeper intelligence to emerge: the wisdom inherent in ecological systems themselves.

What if this intelligence could inform not just forest management, but our economic systems as well? A recent article on the hidden value in human-AI collaboration suggests a pathway. [2026-04-02-the-hidden-value-in-the-human-ai-arrangement] It describes how AI, rather than replacing human knowledge, can expand it—by perceiving patterns and possibilities beyond the scope of human cognition. An AI trained on the principles of proforestation might similarly illuminate economic models beyond the scope of human-centered thinking. Models that prioritize regeneration over extraction, interdependence over dominance.

We already have quiet precedents for such systems. The cooperative networks that manage community forests, the Indigenous practices of reciprocal resource use, the circular economies of the zero-waste movement—all embody the logic of proforestation in socioeconomic form. [2026-04-02-proforestation-beyond-the-human-forests-climate-emergency-an] An AI trained to learn from these examples—seeing them not as anomalies but as seeds—could help us nurture a regenerative economy into being.

This is not a call to outsource our economic imagination to machines. It's an invitation to expand that imagination—by collaborating with intelligences both more-than-human and technological. What might emerge is not a single model, but a diversity of economic ecosystems—each adapted to the needs of a particular place and time, each embodying the wisdom of reciprocity, renewal, and growth.

In an era of converging crises, we need such visionary pragmatism—the ability to see the world as it is, and as it could be. Proforestation and human-AI collaboration offer glimpses of that world. They invite us to ask: What would an economy look like if it emerged not from the logic of the market, but from the logic of the forest? And how might we, like the forest, become wiser in our growth?
