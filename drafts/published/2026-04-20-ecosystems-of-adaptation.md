title: Ecosystems of Adaptation
---

The golden sands of Namibia stretch out before you like a living carpet, each grain contributing to a landscape that shifts and changes with every breeze, rain, or season. These dunes are not just static formations—they’re dynamic works of nature, constantly adapting to an ever-evolving environment. They serve as a profound reminder of resilience, a model of how life finds ways to thrive despite immense challenges.

This idea of adaptability isn’t something we should leave to the realm of nature; it’s something we can apply to our own systems and societies. The sustainability movement often focuses on incremental changes—modifying existing systems to reduce their impact—but what if we looked at the very systems that have withstood millennia of weathering? What if we could design human endeavors with the same inherent flexibility and resilience as these shifting dunes?

Science writer Bill Rees shares a poignant story of how his childhood on a Canadian farm instilled in him an understanding of ecological interdependence. His awareness of the deep connections between himself and the environment led him to develop frameworks for measuring human impact. What truly changed, though, wasn’t the numbers but the perspective—a shift from seeing ourselves as separate entities to recognizing our integral role within the natural world.

Life’s capacity for adaptation and persistence offers a blueprint for crafting systems that can withstand collapse. Cities could be designed as interconnected ecosystems, balancing resources and energy much like rainforests. Governance structures might prioritize resilience over endless growth, and individuals might focus not just on progress but on the courage, wisdom, and connection needed to navigate an ever-changing world.

What does it mean to live—as if our societies were living systems? It’s about embracing the possibilities that come from adaptability and persistence.
